## 1. Purpose

Your first assignment is a **10–15 minute oral presentation** built on one or more review articles provided by the instructors. The aim is **not** to master every omics technology, but to explore this field from your engineering curiosity—understand the problem, the proposed technology, and its potential impact on biomedical engineering or personalised medicine.

## 3. Presentation structure (10–15 min + Q&A)

1. **Personal intro:** why you chose the topic / what you hoped to learn.  
2. **Problem & motivation:** biomedical need addressed by the review.  
3. **Technology overview:** explain key omics concepts in your own words, using analogies if needed.  
4. **Experimental/computational workflow:** include at least one **original diagram** (BioRender, PowerPoint, Figma) showing samples, data flow, and analysis steps.  
5. **Main findings / proposals:** what makes the article novel or disruptive.  
6. **Impact on biomedical engineering:** opportunities, limitations, ethical considerations.  
7. **Open questions / future steps:** what you would investigate next, uncertainties that remain.  
8. **References and figure credits.**

> Be transparent about sections you did not fully understand—describe how you would clarify them. Honesty and curiosity are part of the evaluation.

---

## 4. Preparing the talk

- **Break the reading** into blocks (context, methods, case studies) and summarise in your own words.  
- **Rehearse with a timer** once or twice to stay within 10–15 minutes.  
- **Use guiding questions:** What problem does it solve? What data/sensors does it need? Which steps limit adoption?  
- **Design for clarity:** avoid text-heavy slides, highlight key figures, include your own diagram.  
- **Engage the audience:** finish with 1–2 questions for classmates or the instructor.
