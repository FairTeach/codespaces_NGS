🎯 Objective

In this second challenge, you will design a research project proposal inspired by one or several of the review articles provided.
Your goal is to translate omics and sequencing technologies into a novel application in biomedical research, biotechnology, or personalised medicine.

You will think like a researcher preparing a funding proposal, formulating a scientific hypothesis, a rationale, and an experimental and computational plan with clear impact.

🧩 Assignment Overview

  • Select one (or a combination of up to three) review articles from the following provided list.

🧩 🔗 List of articles to chose

  • Propose a novel research idea applying those concepts to solve a concrete scientific or clinical problem.
  • Write a full project proposal (10–20 pages, excluding references) following a funding-style structure.
  • Prepare a PowerPoint presentation (15-20 minutes) to present and defend your project in class.

📑 Expected Structure of the Proposal

Section Description Suggested Length
1. Title & Abstract A concise, engaging title and a 200–250 word abstract summarising the aim, methodology, and expected impact. ½ page
2. Background and State of the Art Based on the selected review(s). Describe the current knowledge, key technologies, and major open questions. End with the unmet need or gap your project will address. 2–3 pages
3. Research Hypothesis and Rationale Clearly state your central hypothesis. Why is this project important? What biological, technological, or medical challenge does it address? 1 page
4. Objectives and Work Plan List 2–4 specific objectives. Structure them as Work Packages (WPs) with clear aims, methods, deliverables, and milestones. You may include a Gantt chart or flow diagram. 2–3 pages
5. Methodology and Approach Detail the experimental or computational design. Include sample types, datasets, analytical pipelines, model systems, and validation strategies. Mention ethical, regulatory, or reproducibility considerations. 3–4 pages
6. Expected Results and Data Analysis Describe the type of results anticipated (figures, models, datasets). Include analysis strategies, statistical or AI tools, and expected visualisations. 2 pages
7. Innovation and Translational Impact Explain how your proposal advances current knowledge or clinical practice. Highlight novelty, interdisciplinarity, or personalised medicine potential. 1–2 pages
8. Risk Assessment and Contingency Plans Identify key technical, ethical, or data risks. Propose backup strategies. 1 page
9. Team, Resources and Environment Define expertise required, potential collaborations, or infrastructure (laboratory, HPC, bioinformatics, biobank access…). 1 page
10. Ethical, Legal and Societal Implications (ELSI) Discuss data privacy, patient consent, biobank use, and equity aspects relevant to your project. 1 page
11. Expected Impact and Future Perspectives Describe long-term applications, scalability, and potential funding sources (ERC, Horizon Europe, FIS, etc.). 1 page
12. References, Figures and Tables Include all cited references and self-created figures or tables. Use a consistent citation format (APA, Vancouver, or Nature style). —

💡 Example Project Directions

1. Multi-Omic Age Prediction for Preventive Medicine

Based on: Epigenetic ageing clocks… + Genome-scale models in metabologenomics
Integrate DNA methylation and metabolomics data to develop a predictive biological-age model validated in clinical cohorts.

2. Organs-on-Chips for Precision Oncology

Based on: Human organs-on-chips + Spatial transcriptomics
Combine tumour organoid chips with spatial transcriptomics to map therapy resistance mechanisms.

3. Long-Read Transcriptomics to Uncover Hidden Splicing Defects

Based on: Transcriptomics in the era of long-read sequencing + Splicing regulation
Identify pathogenic splicing variants undetectable by short-read RNA-seq.

4. CRISPR Screens for Neurodegeneration Targets

Based on: in vivo CRISPR screening + Neuropathogenesis-on-chips
Discover genetic modifiers of tau or α-synuclein toxicity.

5. Structural Variants and 3D Genome Disruption in Congenital Disease

Based on: Structural variation + 3D genome disease drivers
Integrate Hi-C and optical mapping to link chromatin topology disruption to gene misregulation.

6. Clinical Metagenomics for Antibiotic Stewardship

Based on: Clinical metagenomics + AMR genomic surveillance
Develop a real-time metagenomic workflow to detect antimicrobial resistance genes directly from blood samples.

7. Synthetic Enhancer Circuits for Safe Gene Therapy

Based on: Enhancer modelling + Synthetic genomes
Design synthetic enhancer modules to precisely control therapeutic gene expression.

8. Multi-Omic Prediction of Mental Illness Risk

Based on: Genomics of psychiatric disorders + Epigenetics and individuality
Integrate genome-wide, epigenomic, and transcriptomic data to predict susceptibility to major psychiatric diseases.

🧠 Tips for Success

  • Choose a topic that genuinely interests you — creativity is rewarded.
  • Focus on why and how the technology changes current understanding.
  • Use clear figures (e.g. BioRender or PowerPoint diagrams).
  • Highlight feasibility — imagine this as a real grant submission.
  • Always close with a strong biological and translational interpretation.
